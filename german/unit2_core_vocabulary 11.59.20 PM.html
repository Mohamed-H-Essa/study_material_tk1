Unit 2: Core Vocabulary

The 100 words that matter — grouped by theme, drilled both ways

Your assessment showed solid A1 vocabulary overall, but with specific holes: you confused schwarz (black) with white, and Guten Tag (good day) with good evening. These aren't random mistakes — they're signs that some word groups were learned partially, with gaps where similar-sounding or thematically-related words blur together.

This unit doesn't throw 500 words at you. It gives you exactly 100 high-frequency words across 10 categories. Every word here appears constantly in A1–A2 German. The goal is zero hesitation in both directions: you see the German, you know the English instantly, and vice versa.

How to use this page: Read through each category. Pay attention to the notes — they flag common confusion points and patterns. After all 10 categories, use the flashcard trainer to drill, then take the quiz. The quiz mixes three types: matching, fill-in-the-blank, and odd-one-out.

1. Greetings & Time of Day

This is where you tripped up on the assessment. The issue is simple: Morgen, Tag, and Abend map directly to morning, day, and evening. But when you learned "Guten Tag" as a single chunk meaning "hello," you may have lost the individual word meanings. Let's fix that.

Notice: "Guten" is accusative masculine of "gut" (good). "Gute Nacht" uses feminine because "die Nacht" is feminine. You don't need to worry about why yet — just notice the pattern so it's not random.

The confusion trap: "Guten Tag" is used roughly from 10am to 6pm. "Guten Abend" starts around 6pm. "Guten Morgen" is until about 10am. If you default to "Hallo," you'll never be wrong — but in formal situations (doctor's office, job interview), Germans expect the time-appropriate greeting.

Mnemonic for schwarz: the "schw-" sound is heavy and dark. Think of it like a heavy shadow. "Weiß" sounds like "vice" — think of a white vise grip. Or just remember: Schwarzenegger → "black" + "plowman" (literally "black plowman").

3. Numbers 1–100

You got zwanzig (20) correct and confidently. But numbers in German have a twist that trips up every learner: for 21–99, the ones digit comes BEFORE the tens digit, joined by "und."

25 = fünfundzwanzig → literally "five-and-twenty." Like old English ("four and twenty blackbirds"). You read it backwards compared to how you'd say it in English or Arabic.

Notice the genders follow biology here: male family = der, female family = die. The exceptions are das Kind (child, neuter) and die Eltern / die Geschwister (plural-only words).

Common question: "Hast du Geschwister?" (Do you have siblings?) — "Ja, ich habe einen Bruder und eine Schwester." Note the accusative: einen Bruder (masc.), eine Schwester (fem.).

German plurals are chaotic — Auge→Augen, Hand→Hände (umlaut!), Fuß→Füße (umlaut!). There's no single rule. For now, learn each plural alongside the singular. This is one area where brute-force memorization beats pattern-seeking.

At the doctor: "Mein Rücken tut weh" (my back hurts), "Ich habe Kopfschmerzen" (I have a headache — literally "head-pains"). Useful given your hospital visits.

German loves compound nouns: Schlafzimmer = Schlaf (sleep) + Zimmer (room). Badezimmer = Bad (bath) + Zimmer. Wohnzimmer = Wohn- (living) + Zimmer. The gender is always determined by the LAST word: das Zimmer → das Schlafzimmer, das Wohnzimmer.

Apartment hunting in Germany: You'll see "2-Zimmer-Wohnung" — that means 2-room apartment (bedroom + living room; kitchen and bathroom don't count as Zimmer in listings). Important for when you actually move there.
