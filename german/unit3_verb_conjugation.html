Unit 3: Verb Conjugation — Present Tense

The engine of every sentence — how German verbs change shape for each person

In your assessment, you nailed ich bin confidently. That's the hardest irregular verb in German, and you already own it. The present tense is where you build from here — it's not just "what's happening now" but also "what will happen" (German often uses present tense for future), "what generally happens" (habits), and even "what has been happening" (with seit + present tense).

Every German verb in the dictionary ends in -en (or rarely -n). That's the infinitive: machen (to make), lernen (to learn), sprechen (to speak). To conjugate, you chop off the -en to get the stem, then add the right ending.

The good news: Regular conjugation covers about 80% of German verbs. Learn one pattern and you can conjugate hundreds of verbs. The irregulars that matter are surprisingly few — maybe 20 verbs you'll actually use daily.

1. Regular Conjugation Pattern

Take machen (to make/do). The stem is mach. Now add endings:

PronounEndingFormEnglish
ich-emacheI make
du-stmachstyou make (informal)
er/sie/es-tmachthe/she/it makes
wir-enmachenwe make
ihr-tmachtyou all make
sie/Sie-enmachenthey/you (formal) make
The shortcut that saves you: Only 3 endings to actually memorize: -e (ich), -st (du), and -t (er/sie/es and ihr). The wir and sie/Sie forms are identical to the infinitive — machen. So half the table is free.

This exact pattern works for all of these (and hundreds more):

Common regular verbs

lernen — to learn
spielen — to play
kaufen — to buy
wohnen — to live (reside)
fragen — to ask
sagen — to say
suchen — to search
hören — to hear

Try one: spielen

ich spiele
du spielst
er spielt
wir spielen
ihr spielt
sie spielen

Ich lerne Deutsch. Wir spielen Fußball. Er kauft Brot.
I learn German. We play football. He buys bread.

2. Stem-Changing Verbs

About 50 common verbs change their stem vowel in the du and er/sie/es forms only. The endings stay the same (-st, -t) — it's just the vowel inside the stem that shifts. There are three patterns:

3. The Big Three Irregulars: sein, haben, werden

These are the most important verbs in German. Not because of their own meaning, but because they build other tenses: haben and sein build the Perfekt (past), werden builds the future and the passive. You need them cold.

sein — to be

PronounFormExample
ichbinIch bin müde. (I am tired.)
dubistDu bist nett. (You are nice.)
er/sie/esistEs ist kalt. (It is cold.)
wirsindWir sind hier. (We are here.)
ihrseidIhr seid spät. (You all are late.)
sie/SiesindSie sind Ingenieur. (You are an engineer. [formal])

You already got "ich bin" right on the assessment. Now extend it to all six forms. Notice: wir sind = sie sind = Sie sind — three pronouns, same form.

haben — to have

PronounFormExample
ichhabeIch habe Hunger. (I'm hungry. — lit. "I have hunger")
duhastHast du Zeit? (Do you have time?)
er/sie/eshatEr hat ein Auto. (He has a car.)
wirhabenWir haben ein Problem. (We have a problem.)
ihrhabtHabt ihr Kinder? (Do you all have kids?)
sie/SiehabenSie haben recht. (You are right. [formal])

The irregularity is mild: du hast (drops the b), er hat (drops b and e). Everything else follows the regular pattern. This is the verb you'll use most for Perfekt later: "Ich habe gegessen."

werden — to become (also: will/future)

PronounFormExample
ichwerdeIch werde Ingenieur. (I'm becoming an engineer.)
duwirstDu wirst müde. (You're getting tired.)
er/sie/eswirdEs wird kalt. (It's getting cold.)
wirwerdenWir werden besser. (We're getting better.)
ihrwerdetIhr werdet sehen. (You all will see.)
sie/SiewerdenSie werden kommen. (They will come.)
werden = the Swiss army knife. By itself it means "to become" (Es wird kalt). With an infinitive, it makes the future tense (Ich werde lernen = I will learn). With Partizip II, it makes the passive (Das Buch wird gelesen = The book is being read). You'll encounter all three uses, but for now just learn the conjugation.

4. Spelling Adjustments

Some perfectly regular verbs need a small spelling tweak to stay pronounceable. These aren't irregular — they follow the same endings, just with a helper vowel or a dropped letter.

Stems ending in -t, -d, -chn, -ffn, -gn: add an extra "e"

When the stem ends in a consonant cluster that would be unpronounceable with -st or -t tacked on, German inserts an e as a buffer.

Pronounarbeiten (to work)finden (to find)öffnen (to open)
icharbeitefindeöffne
duarbeitestfindestöffnest
er/sie/esarbeitetfindetöffnet
wirarbeitenfindenöffnen
ihrarbeitetfindetöffnet
sie/Siearbeitenfindenöffnen

Try saying "arbeitst" fast — it's a tongue-twister. "Arbeitest" is pronounceable. That's the entire reason for the extra e. Other common verbs: warten (to wait) → du wartest, antworten (to answer) → du antwortest.

Stems ending in -s, -ß, -z, -x: du form drops the s from -st

Verbdu formWhy
reisen (to travel)du reistreisST would double the S sound
heißen (to be called)du heißtheißST is unpronounceable
sitzen (to sit)du sitztsitzST is a mess

This is purely phonetic — German avoids hissing consonant pileups. The result: du reist looks like er reist. Context tells you which is which.

5. What Present Tense Actually Covers

German present tense does more work than English present tense. It covers four situations:

A. Current actions (like English)

Ich lerne Deutsch.
I'm learning German. / I learn German.

German doesn't distinguish between "I learn" and "I am learning" — both are just "Ich lerne." There is no "-ing" form in German.

B. Future (with time word)

Ich fahre morgen nach Berlin.
I'm going to Berlin tomorrow.

No need for "werde" (will) here. The time word "morgen" makes the future meaning clear. This is how most Germans express near-future plans — it sounds more natural than using werden.

C. Ongoing states (seit + present tense)

Ich wohne seit drei Jahren in Kairo.
I have been living in Cairo for three years.
This trips up every English speaker. English uses present perfect ("have been living") for actions that started in the past and continue now. German uses present tense + seit (since/for). "Ich lerne seit einem Jahr Deutsch" = "I have been learning German for one year." NOT "Ich habe gelernt" — that would mean you stopped.

D. General truths / habits

Die Sonne geht im Osten auf.
The sun rises in the east.

Questions: just invert

To make a yes/no question, swap the subject and verb:

Du sprichst Deutsch. → Sprichst du Deutsch?
You speak German. → Do you speak German?

No "do" helper needed. Just flip the order. This is one thing German does more simply than English.

du vs. ihr vs. Sie — which "you"?

du — one person, informal

Friends, family, kids, colleagues you're close with. You'll use this most.

ihr — multiple people, informal

"You guys / you all." Talking to a group of friends.

Sie — formal (one or many)

Strangers, officials, your boss (initially). Always capitalized. Conjugates like sie (they).

The rule in Germany

Default to Sie with strangers. Most workplaces switch to du quickly. When in doubt, match what they use with you.
