01 RDS — Multi-AZ, read replicas & the relational toolbox

Two features that look alike and solve opposite problems

Half the relational questions on this exam hinge on one distinction: do you need to survive a failure, or do you need to serve more reads? Those are different tools.

Multi-AZRead replicas
Jobhigh availability / failoverscale read throughput
Replicationsynchronous to a standbyasynchronous (lag possible)
Serves reads?No (classic Multi-AZ standby is idle)Yes — that's the point
Failoverautomatic, endpoint swaps (~60–120s)manual promote to standalone
Spananother AZ, same Regionsame Region, cross-Region, or cross-account

Key fact — they combine, and there's a newer option

Multi-AZ and read replicas are orthogonal — production systems often run both. And the Multi-AZ DB cluster deployment (1 writer + 2 readable standbys across 3 AZs) gives you HA and readable secondaries with faster failover — the answer when a stem wants "automatic failover but the standbys shouldn't sit idle."

The rest of the RDS kit:

  • RDS Proxy — pools connections (saves you from Lambda connection storms and "too many connections"), enforces IAM auth, and cuts failover time significantly.
  • Encrypt an existing unencrypted DB — you can't do it in place: snapshot → copy the snapshot with encryption (KMS) → restore a new encrypted instance.
  • Point-in-time recovery — automated backups let you restore to any second within the retention window (up to 35 days). Manual snapshots live until you delete them and can be copied cross-Region.
  • Cross-Region read replica — local low-latency reads and a promote-able DR copy in another Region.

// field note — the most common RDS mistake

The AWS forums are full of people who switched on Multi-AZ expecting more read capacity and got a standby that refuses to answer a single SELECT. Multi-AZ buys a standby that takes over when things break; read replicas buy copies that answer reads. If you can keep "failover vs scaling" straight, you'll out-answer most candidates on the relational questions alone.

02 Aurora & Aurora Global Database

Where your headline miss lives

Aurora rebuilt the relational engine on a distributed storage fabric. The exam-critical part is its global story — and the difference between a planned and an unplanned regional failover.

Aurora in one paragraph

Aurora (MySQL/PostgreSQL-compatible) keeps six copies of your data across three AZs in a self-healing storage layer that the database instances share. Because a failover doesn't copy any data, promoting one of the up-to-15 Aurora Replicas takes well under a minute — and those replicas serve reads (via the reader endpoint) at the same time. Aurora Serverless autoscales capacity for variable or intermittent workloads with little to manage. Backtrack (Aurora MySQL) rewinds a cluster to a past moment in seconds without a restore.

Key fact — Aurora Global Database failover

A Global Database has one read/write primary Region and one or more read-only secondary Regions, with storage-level replication (typical lag < 1s → RPO ~1s, RTO < 1min). How you fail over depends on why:

  • Managed planned failover — for planned events (DR drills, Region rotation) while the primary is healthy. It promotes a secondary and keeps the global topology (the old primary becomes a secondary).
  • Detach & promote (unplanned) — during an actual primary-Region outage, you remove a secondary from the Global Database and promote it to a standalone read/write cluster. This breaks the topology, but it's the path when the primary is gone.

The Mock 2 trap, named

For automatic DNS failover to the DR Region, the answer is Route 53 active-passive failover routing with health checks — not latency-based routing. Latency routing sends each user to the nearest healthy endpoint; it's a performance tool, not a "promote the standby when the primary dies" tool. Pairing two routing policies (as the tempting wrong answer did) is double-bait.

// field note — Aurora's quiet rebuild

When AWS shipped Aurora (GA 2015) it didn't just wrap MySQL — it tore out the storage engine and replaced it with a distributed, self-healing fabric spread across three AZs. Decoupling compute from that shared storage is why a replica can be promoted in seconds and why you can attach 15 readers without copying the database 15 times. AWS has called Aurora one of the fastest-growing services in its history.

03 DynamoDB, caching & purpose-built data

Match the access pattern to the engine

When the question describes the shape of the data and the latency target, it's telling you the answer. This is also where your caching wins live — let's lock them in.

DynamoDB

Serverless NoSQL key-value/document store with single-digit-millisecond latency at any scale (microseconds with DAX). Choose on-demand capacity for spiky/unpredictable traffic, provisioned + auto scaling for steady/predictable load. Global Tables give multi-Region active-active read/write; Streams emit change events (→ Lambda) for CDC; TTL auto-expires items. Reach for DynamoDB when key-based access and predictable latency matter more than relational joins.

Caching: ElastiCache & DAX

ChoiceUse it for
ElastiCache RedisHA caching with replication, Multi-AZ failover, persistence; rich structures — sorted sets for leaderboards, pub/sub, geospatial
ElastiCache Memcachedsimplest multi-threaded cache; no persistence, replication, or Multi-AZ
DAXmicrosecond cache specifically in front of DynamoDB, via the DynamoDB API (not a general cache)
MemoryDB for Rediswhen you need durable Redis as a primary database, not just a cache

Caching strategies: lazy loading / cache-aside populates the cache on a miss (only cache what's asked for; bound staleness with a TTL); write-through updates the cache on every write (always fresh, more writes). Pair lazy loading with a TTL when source data changes on a known cadence.

Redshift & the purpose-built tour

Redshift is a columnar, massively-parallel data warehouse for analytics/OLAP over huge datasets — not an OLTP database for high-concurrency single-row transactions. The purpose-built one-liners: Neptune = graph (relationships), DocumentDB = MongoDB-compatible, Keyspaces = managed Cassandra, Timestream = time-series/IoT, QLDB = immutable ledger.

// field note — where DynamoDB came from

DynamoDB's ancestor, "Dynamo," was built inside Amazon after relational databases buckled under holiday-peak shopping-cart load. The famous 2007 Dynamo paper made a deliberate trade: give up a little consistency to keep the cart always writeable, because a customer who can't add to their cart is lost revenue. That paper influenced Cassandra and Riak; the managed service launched in 2012. The exam lesson: DynamoDB is for predictable single-digit-ms latency at scale — not for joins. And the old engineering line still bites here: one of the two hard problems in computing is cache invalidation, which is exactly why every cache answer comes with a TTL.
