01 High availability & graceful failure

Assume everything breaks, then design so it doesn't matter

Resilient architecture is mostly the absence of single points of failure: spread across AZs, put a load balancer and an Auto Scaling group in front, decouple the tiers, and keep instances stateless so losing one is a non-event.

The HA checklist the exam rewards

  • Multi-AZ everything — ASG spanning AZs behind an ELB, Multi-AZ databases, multi-AZ subnets. One AZ dying should be invisible.
  • Stateless instances — externalize session state (ElastiCache/DynamoDB) so any instance can serve any request and scale-in/deploys lose nothing.
  • Decouple — a queue (SQS) between tiers means a slow or failed downstream backs up instead of dropping work.
  • ELB health checks on the ASG — so a broken-but-running instance gets replaced, not left in rotation.
  • Automate recovery — Auto Scaling self-heals; don't rely on a human noticing.

Key fact — gray failures & ARC

A gray failure is a partial, ambiguous impairment: health checks pass, but latency or error rates quietly climb. Amazon Application Recovery Controller (ARC) gives you the tools the standard health check can't:

  • Zonal shift / autoshift — drain a suspect AZ with one auto-expiring action (manual), or let AWS do it on impairment signals you can't see (autoshift), validated by practice runs.
  • Routing controls — highly-available on/off switches (data plane spread across multiple Regions) to trigger a Region-level failover reliably, even mid-outage when control-plane APIs are shaky.
  • Readiness checks — continuously verify your recovery environment is actually scaled and configured to take over.

// field note — "everything fails, all the time"

That line, widely attributed to Amazon CTO Werner Vogels, is the whole philosophy in five words. The corollary lesson came on February 28, 2017: a typo during a routine debugging command removed far more S3 capacity than intended in us-east-1, cascading across dozens of services. The kicker — the AWS status dashboard itself depended on S3, so for a while it couldn't even show that S3 was down. The takeaway runs through this whole file: hard Regional dependencies are single points of failure, and resilience is a design choice you make before the incident.

02 Route 53 — routing policies & DNS failover

Seven policies; pick the one the requirement names

Almost every Route 53 question is "performance vs failover vs compliance vs split." Match the intent and you're done.

PolicyUse it when the stem says…
Simpleone record, no health-based decision needed
Weightedsplit traffic by percentage — canary, blue/green, gradual shift
Latency-basedsend users to the lowest-latency Region (performance, active-active)
Failoveractive-passive DR — primary + standby with health checks
Geolocationroute by user location — compliance, data residency, localization
Geoproximityroute by distance with a tunable bias (via Traffic Flow)
Multivalue answerreturn several healthy records for simple client-side HA

Failover ≠ latency

For automatic DR cutover to a standby, the answer is failover routing (active-passive, health-checked). Latency-based routing is a performance tool — it sends each user to the nearest healthy Region; it is not "promote the standby when the primary dies." Choosing two routing policies at once, or latency where failover belongs, is a recurring trap (and the one that cost you on Mock 2).

Two more reflexes: an alias record is Route 53-specific — it can sit at the zone apex (example.com), points to AWS resources (ELB, CloudFront, S3, API GW), is free, and can evaluate target health; a plain CNAME cannot be used at the apex. And DNS failover speed is bounded by the record's TTL — resolvers cache the old answer until it expires, so DNS-based failover is "minutes," not "seconds" (which is exactly why Global Accelerator exists for sub-minute Regional failover — Section 03).

// trivia

The "53" in Route 53 is the TCP/UDP port number for DNS — a quiet nod to what the service does. AWS also backs Route 53 with a 100% availability SLA, one of the few services it commits to that level for, because DNS resolution failing is catastrophic in a way few other failures are.

03 Disaster recovery strategies & global delivery

RTO and RPO choose the strategy — and the cost

This is your headline miss. The four DR strategies sit on one axis: faster recovery costs more. Read the RTO/RPO in the stem and the answer is decided.

Key fact — RPO vs RTO

RPO = how much data you can afford to lose (the gap back to the last good copy) → it drives how often you replicate. RTO = how much downtime you can tolerate (time to be running again) → it drives how much standby infrastructure you keep warm. "Lose at most 5 minutes of data" is an RPO; "be back within 1 hour" is an RTO.

StrategyWhat's running in DRRTO / RPOCost
Backup & Restorenothing — just backups in another Regionhourslowest
Pilot Lightcore data replicated; minimal/off computetens of minuteslow
Warm Standbya scaled-down but fully functional copyminutesmedium
Multi-Site Active-Activefull production in multiple Regionsnear zerohighest

The deciding clue is what's already running. "Just keep the data safe, hours of downtime is fine" → backup & restore. "Database is live-replicated but compute is off until disaster" → pilot light. "A smaller-but-complete environment is always up and we scale it on failover" → warm standby. "Both Regions serve live traffic, near-zero downtime" → multi-site active-active.

// field note — DR is a spectrum, not a switch

AWS's own disaster-recovery guidance lays these four strategies out on a single dial from cheapest/slowest to priciest/instant. The architect's job isn't to pick "the best" one — it's to read the business's tolerance for lost data and downtime and choose the cheapest strategy that still meets it. Spending warm-standby money on a workload that's fine with an overnight restore is as wrong on the exam as the reverse.
